Surge reference — load types
Resistive — 1×
LED bulbs, rice cooker, stove, water heater. No inrush. Safest for inverters.
Electronic — 1.2–1.5×
TVs, laptops, chargers, routers. Brief capacitor inrush. Low risk.
Inductive Motor — 2–4×
Electric fans, inverter aircon. 0.1–0.5s surge at startup. Inverter must handle momentary spike.
Compressor — 3–7×
Fridges, non-inverter aircon, pumps. Locked-rotor inertia. Most dangerous for inverter sizing.
Magnetron / HV Transformer — 5–8×
Microwave ovens. 1200W microwave draws up to 8,400W for 50–200ms at startup. 2kW inverter WILL trip. Need inverter surge rating ≥ startup watts.
Inverter must be sized: (worst surge watts) + (all others running simultaneously). Never start two high-surge appliances at the same time.

Add industrial loads. System sizes inverter for worst surge + all others running simultaneously.
Industrial surge types — critical differences
Inverter Welder — 8×–10× surge + DC Leakage
Creates BOTH massive inrush AND smooth DC leakage current in the AC line. Standard Type A RCBOs are "blinded" by DC leakage — they cannot trip and CANNOT protect. Type B RCBO is mandatory. Low-frequency transformer inverter absorbs arc spikes that destroy HF inverters.
Jackhammer / Compressor — 7×–10× (Curve D required)
High-torque induction motor against maximum mechanical resistance at startup. Standard Curve C breakers trip at 5×–10×. Curve D allows 10×–14× without tripping. Without Curve D, breaker trips on every startup — work stops every 5 minutes.
3C Battery rule: 400Ah × 3C = 1,200A continuous. At 48V: 1,200A × 48V = 57.6kW available burst from battery. The battery is the "burst reservoir" that prevents voltage sag during welding arc.
System parameters — 48V industrial fixed
For welding loads — 3C POWER batteries, not standard storage batteries. Standard residential LFP batteries have 0.5C–1C discharge. A 200A welder pulling 100A from a 48V bank at 0.5C would require a 200Ah bank just to stay within spec. You need 3C cells with active thermal management and 200A–300A high-discharge BMS.

4/0 AWG (95mm²) — Pure Oxygen-Free Copper MANDATORY
NEVER use CCA (Copper-Clad Aluminum) cables for battery connections. At 3C discharge (300A+), CCA cables will overheat, melt insulation, and cause fire. CCA has ~60% of copper's conductivity — at high current it heats rapidly. Use only 100% pure oxygen-free copper (OFC) for all battery and busbar connections.

DC MCCB — Main Battery Protection (250A–400A)
Why DC MCCB not ANL fuse? ANL fuses are one-time use — blown in a workshop fault = work stops until replaced. A DC MCCB is resettable after fault is cleared. MCCBs also handle sustained high-amp heat better than fuse elements under 3C cycling. MUST be DC-rated — AC MCCBs cannot extinguish 48V DC arcs.

Ferrite Core EMI/RFI — Welding Arc Noise Suppression
Welding arc generates broadband EMI from 1kHz to 300MHz. Without ferrite cores: CCTV corrupts during welding, WiFi drops to zero, sensitive electronics malfunction. Three positions cover the complete noise path.
Position 1: Inverter AC Output
Large toroidal ferrite ring at inverter AC output — L+N wound 3 turns through core. Stops inverter switching noise (16–100kHz) entering distribution board. Brands: Fair-Rite 5977002701, Würth 74270010, TDK ZCAT.
Position 2: Welder Power Cord
2–3 snap-on ferrites on welder's power cord, within 10cm of welder inlet. Multiple cores stacked for max attenuation. Traps arc-generated EMI at source before it enters shared wiring.
Position 3: DC Battery Cables
Large-bore snap-on ferrites (25mm+) on DC cables from battery to inverter. MPPT switching frequency creates its own noise that can interfere with welder arc control electronics. 4 pcs minimum on DC side.
Industrial 48V — Common Busbar Architecture. All batteries connect to a central 600A copper busbar. MCCB sits between busbar and inverter — this is the correct industrial design for 3C high-discharge systems.
